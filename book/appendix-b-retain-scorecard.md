---

The following scorecard applies the RETAIN model (Gunter, Kenny & Vick, 2008) to each game on the platform. Scores use a four-point scale: **0** (absent), **1** (weak), **2** (moderate), **3** (strong). Scores are qualitative assessments based on the case study analyses in Part II, not quantitative measurements.

## Score Interpretations

**17–18: Exemplary integration.** Math Investigator and Target Numbers are the platform's strongest designs by this measure. Both achieve high scores across all six dimensions, reflecting the depth of their learning integration and the quality of their scaffolding.

**14–15: Strong design with specific gaps.** Five in a Row, Number Line, Math Blocks, Math Blocks II, and Rumble are all strong designs with identifiable improvement opportunities — primarily in Adaptation (limited within-level adaptive response) and Naturalization/Transfer depending on the game's design philosophy.

**11–12: Adequate but limited.** Capture Squares, New Math App, and Fraction Scouts are functional educational tools that achieve their basic objectives but have more significant design gaps — particularly in Transfer and Adaptation — that limit their educational effectiveness beyond immediate practice.

---

## Dimension-Level Observations

**Embedding** is the platform's strongest collective dimension. Eight of ten games score 3; the two that score 2 (Capture Squares, Fraction Scouts) are the platform's most exogenously integrated games. This reflects the platform designer's genuine understanding of what makes educational game design distinctive.

**Adaptation** is the platform's weakest collective dimension. No game scores 3; eight score 2 and two score 1. This reflects the platform-wide absence of adaptive difficulty systems. All games offer fixed difficulty tiers; none dynamically responds to learner performance. This is the platform's single highest-leverage improvement opportunity.

**Relevance** and **Transfer** are strong where real-world contextualisation is present (Math Investigator's story problems, Math Blocks' authentic manipulative design, Number Line's foundational representational role) and weaker where the game world is abstract (Capture Squares' arbitrary numbers, New Math App's decontextualised operations).
