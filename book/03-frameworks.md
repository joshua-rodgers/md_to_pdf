# Chapter 3: Frameworks for Evaluating Educational Games

---

The learning theories in Chapter 2 describe how learning works. This chapter introduces the design and evaluation frameworks that translate those theories into practical criteria for assessing whether a specific game design is likely to produce learning.

Three frameworks are central to the analyses in Part II. They are complementary, not redundant: each captures something the others miss.

---

## 3.1 The MDA Framework

The MDA Framework — Mechanics, Dynamics, Aesthetics — was developed by Robin Hunicke, Marc LeBlanc, and Robert Zubek as a formal tool for game design and analysis, presented at the Game Developers Conference in 2004. It remains the most widely used analytical framework in academic game studies.

The framework formalises game design into three interrelated layers:

**Mechanics** are the rules, data representations, and algorithms — the formal specification of the game as a system. Mechanics are what the designer builds. They include things like: the player can place pieces on a grid; the game ends when a player has five pieces in a row; the timer counts down from three minutes; the player earns one point per correct answer.

**Dynamics** are the run-time behaviour of the mechanics as players interact with them — the emergent gameplay. The same mechanics can produce radically different dynamics depending on how players respond to them. Mechanics are designed; dynamics are experienced. The dynamics of a game are often surprising even to its designer, because they emerge from the interaction of mechanics with human behaviour rather than being directly specified.

**Aesthetics** are the emotional responses and experiential qualities the game evokes in its players — the "fun." Hunicke, LeBlanc, and Zubek identified eight aesthetic qualities in games: sensation (sensory pleasure), fantasy (make-believe), narrative (drama), challenge, fellowship (social connection), discovery, expression, and submission (pastime/relaxation). The goal of game design, in the MDA view, is to produce desired aesthetics through the mechanics that generate appropriate dynamics.

Crucially, designers and players experience the framework from opposite ends. Designers think from mechanics toward aesthetics: these mechanics will produce these dynamics, which will produce these experiences. Players experience from aesthetics toward mechanics: they have an experience, which emerges from gameplay dynamics, which are generated by mechanics they may not consciously notice.

For educational games, the MDA framework has been extended with a fourth layer: **Learning Objectives**. In the extended model, mechanics must not only generate aesthetics; they must be aligned with specific, measurable learning goals. A well-designed educational game generates desired aesthetics through mechanics that also structurally instantiate the target mathematics. A poorly designed one achieves one at the expense of the other — either fun without learning, or learning without engagement.

Throughout Part II, the MDA lens is applied as follows: What are this game's mechanics? What dynamics do they produce? What aesthetics do players experience? And how well are those mechanics aligned with the mathematical learning objectives the game claims to address?

## 3.2 The RETAIN Model

The RETAIN Model was developed by Gunter, Kenny, and Vick in research published in *Educational Technology Research and Development* (2008). It provides six evaluation criteria specifically designed to assess how effectively educational content is embedded in game mechanics — and, by implication, a design checklist for games being developed.

The six components are:

**Relevance** — Does the content connect meaningfully to learners' lives and existing knowledge? Do game levels connect logically to each other and to real-world mathematical contexts? Relevance in mathematics games often takes the form of contextualisation: not "what is 7 × 8?" but "if you have seven bags with eight apples in each, how many apples do you have?" The context does not need to be elaborate to be relevant; it needs to give the mathematical operation a reason for existing.

**Embedding** — Is the learning content genuinely embedded in the game mechanics, or merely attached to them? This component is most directly related to the endogenous/exogenous fantasy distinction from Chapter 1. A game with high embedding is one in which the mathematics is not a prerequisite for playing but is constitutive of playing — you do mathematics *by* playing, not *before* you can play. Games with low embedding present mathematics as an obstacle that must be cleared to access the game.

**Transfer** — Does the game design support transfer of learning to new contexts and problem types? Transfer is the hardest criterion to meet, and it is the one most frequently tested in the research literature. A game can produce impressive performance within its own mechanics while failing to support any generalisation of the underlying mathematics. The design features most associated with transfer are: varied contexts for the same mathematical operations, explicit conceptual scaffolding rather than pure procedure, and reflection prompts that invite learners to articulate what they have understood.

**Adaptation** — Does the game support the learning processes of assimilation and accommodation described by Piaget? This criterion asks whether the game builds a progression that allows new material to connect with and extend existing schemas, rather than presenting each level as an isolated challenge with no relationship to what came before.

**Immersion** — Is the player intellectually engaged and invested? Immersion in the RETAIN sense is not visual or narrative immersion — it is the cognitive and motivational engagement described by flow theory. A learner who is immersed in the RETAIN sense is attending fully to the mathematical challenge, not to the visual decoration around it.

**Naturalization** — Does the game support the development of fluency — the point at which mathematical operations become automatic enough that they no longer consume significant working memory? Automaticity is the goal of arithmetic fact practice: when 7 × 8 is retrieved from memory instantly, it no longer consumes the working memory that would otherwise be available for the higher-order reasoning required to use that fact in a problem-solving context.

In the case study chapters, RETAIN scores are applied qualitatively — as a structured checklist that identifies specific design strengths and weaknesses — rather than numerically. A consolidated scorecard for all games is provided in Appendix B.

## 3.3 Plass, Homer, and Kinzer's Multidimensional Model

In their 2015 paper "Foundations of Game-Based Learning" in *Educational Psychologist*, Jan Plass, Bruce Homer, and Charles Kinzer proposed the most comprehensive theoretical synthesis of game-based learning research to date. Their multidimensional model argues that effective educational games must address four dimensions of engagement simultaneously.

**Cognitive engagement** supports schema construction, problem-solving, and transfer. It corresponds to the constructivist and CLT principles from Chapter 2: the game must provide the right kind and amount of challenge, manage working memory load appropriately, and offer feedback that builds understanding rather than just confirming correctness.

**Behavioural engagement** structures actions, responses, and practice. This dimension is about the physical and procedural experience of playing: the interface must be responsive, the actions must be legible, the consequences of actions must be clear. In children's games specifically, behavioural engagement is closely related to motor control and interface accessibility.

**Affective engagement** induces positive emotions that broaden cognitive resources. This draws on Barbara Fredrickson's Broaden-and-Build theory of positive emotions: positive emotional states — curiosity, delight, pride in accomplishment — broaden the range of thoughts and actions a learner can access, making them more cognitively flexible and more likely to persist. Games that produce frustration, shame, or anxiety have the opposite effect: they narrow cognitive resources and trigger avoidance.

**Sociocultural engagement** leverages social interaction, collaboration, and cultural relevance. The most consistently strong finding in the GBL research literature is that collaborative game elements are associated with better learning outcomes. Games that allow players to learn with or from each other — even in simple ways, like displaying a collaborative leaderboard or enabling turn-taking — produce measurably stronger learning than identical single-player experiences.

The Plass-Homer-Kinzer model is used throughout Part II as a completeness check: does this game address all four dimensions, or does it optimise for one or two at the expense of the others?

## 3.4 How These Frameworks Work Together

The three frameworks are not redundant; they illuminate different aspects of the same design problem.

The MDA framework is most useful for analysing the *structure* of a game: what is the player actually doing, and does that activity generate the intended experience? It is the framework to reach for when asking "is this actually a game?" or "does the fun emerge from the mathematics or despite it?"

The RETAIN model is most useful for evaluating the *quality of learning integration*: is the content genuinely embedded, or bolted on? Does the design support transfer, or only performance? It is the framework to reach for when asking "will this game produce learning that lasts and generalises?"

The Plass-Homer-Kinzer model is most useful for assessing *completeness*: does the game address the full range of what effective educational game design requires? It is the framework to reach for when asking "what has this design prioritised, and at what cost?"

In combination, the three frameworks provide a reasonably comprehensive lens for the design analyses that follow.

## 3.5 A Note on Applying Frameworks to Real Games

Frameworks are tools, not verdicts. Their purpose in this book is to structure analysis and identify specific, actionable design questions — not to generate scores that declare a game "good" or "bad."

The games in this platform are, without exception, functional educational products made by an educator who understands the subject matter and cares about the learners using them. The analytical questions raised by applying the RETAIN model or the MDA framework are not criticisms of intent; they are observations about design decisions, some of which could be made differently.

The dual-expert perspective in Part II is designed to resist the tendency toward either uncritical enthusiasm or reflexive critique. Where a game design decision is supported by evidence, it will be acknowledged as such. Where a decision represents a genuine missed opportunity, it will be named as that. The goal throughout is to be useful to someone who wants to make these games better.
