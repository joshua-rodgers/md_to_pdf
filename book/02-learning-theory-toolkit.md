# Chapter 2: The Learning Theory Toolkit

---

Good educational game design is applied learning science. This chapter establishes the theoretical vocabulary that will be used throughout the case studies in Part II. Readers already fluent in these frameworks may treat this chapter as a reference; readers new to educational game design research will find it an essential foundation.

Six frameworks matter most to this analysis. They are not independent — they cross-reference, qualify, and in some places contradict each other — but together they provide a reasonably complete account of how learning works and what game design must do to support it.

---

## 2.1 Constructivism at the Core

Constructivism is the foundational epistemological position underlying most modern educational practice and virtually all research-based educational game design. Its core claim is that learners do not passively receive knowledge; they actively construct it through experience, interaction, and reflection.

Jean Piaget's cognitive constructivism holds that knowledge is organised into mental schemas — structured representations of what we know — and that learning occurs through two complementary processes: *assimilation* (fitting new information into existing schemas) and *accommodation* (modifying existing schemas when new information cannot fit). For educational game designers, the implication is that games must be designed to connect to what learners already know while providing experiences that productively challenge and extend those schemas.

Lev Vygotsky's social constructivism adds a crucial dimension: learning is fundamentally social. Knowledge is constructed in interaction with others, and the most productive learning happens in what Vygotsky called the *Zone of Proximal Development* (ZPD) — the gap between what a learner can accomplish independently and what they can accomplish with guidance or support. Games that are set at exactly the level of current independent competence are insufficiently challenging; games that demand performance at the outer boundary of the ZPD, with appropriate support, produce genuine development.

The ZPD concept generates the most practically important structural principle in educational game design: *scaffolding*, a term introduced by Jerome Bruner. Scaffolding is temporary support that enables a learner to accomplish tasks they could not manage alone, with the expectation that the support is progressively reduced — *faded* — as the learner's independent competence develops. In game terms, scaffolding includes tutorials, hints, reduced difficulty on initial exposure, and safety mechanisms that prevent early failure from becoming discouraging. Effective scaffolding does not make the game easier; it makes the challenge appropriately calibrated to the learner's current position in their ZPD.

The constructivist position also implies that discovery learning — learning through exploration rather than direct instruction — is often valuable. But it is important to note the tension here, which will recur throughout this book: constructivism and Cognitive Load Theory are not always in agreement about how much unguided discovery is productive, particularly for novice learners.

## 2.2 Cognitive Load Theory

John Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (CLT), developed in the late 1980s, is arguably the most practically consequential contribution to instructional design in the past half-century. Its core insight is that human working memory is severely limited — Miller's foundational 1956 research established that working memory can hold approximately seven items (plus or minus two) at once — and that learning is the process of encoding information into long-term memory as organised schemas. Anything that consumes working memory resources without contributing to schema formation impedes learning.

CLT identifies three types of cognitive load:

**Intrinsic load** is the inherent complexity of the content itself — the number of elements that must be processed simultaneously to understand the material. For a child learning single-digit addition, intrinsic load is relatively low; for a child learning multi-digit subtraction with regrouping, it is considerably higher. Intrinsic load cannot be reduced without changing the content, but it can be managed through sequencing: teaching prerequisite concepts first, so that later material builds on established schemas rather than requiring everything to be held in working memory simultaneously.

**Extraneous load** is cognitive load generated by poor design — irrelevant information, confusing layout, unnecessary visual complexity, redundant audio. Extraneous load competes with the cognitive work of learning without contributing to it. Reducing extraneous load is the primary design obligation CLT places on educational software developers. Every animation that does not serve the learning objective, every sound effect that distracts rather than signals, every visual element that must be processed before the learner can engage with the mathematics — these are extraneous load generators, and their accumulation is the most common mechanism by which well-intentioned educational games fail.

**Germane load** is cognitive load devoted to schema formation — the productive mental effort of connecting new material to existing knowledge and building new mental structures. Germane load is what learning actually consists of. Good educational game design maximises germane load while minimising extraneous load and managing intrinsic load through careful sequencing.

The CLT framework has a specific and important implication for children's educational games: young learners have smaller working memory capacity than adults, and the gap between what their working memory can handle and what an uncareful designer might demand of it is substantial. A game that an adult designer finds "simple" may be overwhelming for a six-year-old whose working memory is simultaneously processing the game mechanics, the visual interface, the audio feedback, the task instructions, and the mathematical content.

## 2.3 Flow Theory

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's theory of flow — developed from his research in the 1970s and presented in full in *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience* (1990) — describes a psychological state of complete absorption in a challenging activity. In flow, action and awareness merge; self-consciousness recedes; time distorts. The state is intrinsically rewarding.

Flow occurs within a specific and narrow relationship between challenge and skill. Csikszentmihalyi's three-channel model is the relevant frame for game designers: when challenge substantially exceeds skill, the result is anxiety; when skill substantially exceeds challenge, the result is boredom; only when challenge is slightly above current skill — demanding just a bit more than the learner can comfortably manage — does flow become possible.

The implications for game design are direct. A game that is too easy for a given learner is boring. A game that is too hard is anxiety-producing. A game that maintains appropriate challenge as the learner's skill increases keeps the learner in the flow channel. This is why adaptive difficulty adjustment (DDA) — dynamically modifying game difficulty in response to learner performance — is considered such a powerful mechanism in educational game design: it is the technical implementation of keeping players in the flow channel.

Jenova Chen's foundational MFA thesis "Flow in Games" (2006) operationalised flow theory for interactive media design, establishing dynamic difficulty as the central mechanism for flow maintenance and demonstrating that players' enjoyment and persistence correlate strongly with their time spent in the flow channel. The *EGameFlow* evaluation model (Fu, Su & Yu, 2009) subsequently adapted this framework specifically for educational games, identifying flow as a key predictor of both engagement and learning.

The practically important point for the analyses in Part II is this: a game with fixed difficulty levels can only be in the flow channel for learners at a specific level of current competence. For learners above or below that level, it will produce boredom or anxiety. Most of the games on the platform under analysis use tiered difficulty levels (typically three to seven stages) rather than dynamic difficulty adjustment. This is a manageable limitation when the tiers are well-calibrated and learners can self-select — but it creates systematic problems when the calibration is off, when advancement is automatic rather than mastery-gated, or when the learner population spans a wide range of ability levels within a single tier.

## 2.4 Self-Determination Theory

Edward Deci and Richard Ryan's Self-Determination Theory (SDT), developed from the early 1970s and systematically presented in Ryan and Deci (2000), is the dominant motivational framework in social science and the one most extensively applied to educational game design research. Its core argument is that human motivation exists on a continuum from external regulation to intrinsic motivation, and that well-being and sustained engagement require the satisfaction of three basic psychological needs.

The three needs are:

**Autonomy** — the sense that one's actions are self-chosen and self-directed. In game design, autonomy is supported by meaningful choice: when the player genuinely decides what to do next, not merely selects from a menu of functionally identical options. Autonomy is undermined by controlling game structures — mandatory tutorials that cannot be skipped, restricted progression, punishment for deviation from expected paths.

**Competence** — the sense of being effective and capable. In game design, competence is supported by appropriate challenge, clear feedback, and mastery experiences. The flow channel, in SDT terms, is the region where challenge is calibrated to support competence experience without triggering incompetence anxiety. Competence is undermined by repeated failure, tasks far beyond current ability, and success that feels trivially easy.

**Relatedness** — the sense of connection to others. In single-player educational games, relatedness is harder to design for, but it can be achieved through narrative connection (a character the player cares about), competitive or collaborative elements, and the social context of game use (playing alongside classmates or a parent).

SDT's organismic integration theory describes the process by which external motivation becomes increasingly internalised. A child who plays a math game only because their teacher requires it is externally regulated. A child who plays because they want to beat their previous score is introjected. A child who plays because they have personally identified with getting better at mathematics is in a state of identification. A child who plays because mathematical competence has become a core part of who they understand themselves to be is integrated. The goal of educational game design — and what distinguishes lasting learning from temporary performance — is to move learners along this continuum toward integration and intrinsic motivation.

The SDT research has an important implication for gamification, which will be examined in Chapter 4: reward structures that satisfy competence and relatedness needs while preserving autonomy can support movement along the motivational continuum. Reward structures that are purely controlling — that reduce the learner's sense of self-determination — do not.

## 2.5 Spaced Repetition and the Forgetting Curve

Hermann Ebbinghaus's nineteenth-century research on memory established a fundamental and troubling fact about how humans learn and forget: without reinforcement, newly learned information is forgotten in a predictable exponential curve. Roughly half of new information is lost within an hour; by 24 hours, most of what was "learned" in a single session is gone.

The practical response to this finding — spaced repetition — schedules review at algorithmically determined intervals that force retrieval just before forgetting occurs, progressively extending the interval with each successful recall. Spaced repetition is empirically the most effective known technique for long-term retention of factual material, including arithmetic facts.

The tension this creates for game design is real: spaced repetition is inherently repetitive, and repetition is the enemy of moment-to-moment engagement. A game that implements spaced repetition faithfully will present learners with previously encountered material, which violates the novelty principle that drives much game-based engagement.

The research-informed resolution is to provide varied contexts for spaced review — different game formats that present the same underlying mathematical content in structurally distinct ways — and to embed spaced repetition logic in a progression system that makes the review feel like a natural part of advancing through the game rather than a mandated drill.

The platform under analysis does not implement explicit spaced repetition across its games. Each game is a standalone experience; there is no inter-game tracking of mastery that would enable algorithmically scheduled review. This is a significant gap in the platform's long-term learning architecture, one we will return to in Chapter 17.

## 2.6 Feedback: The Central Mechanism

Feedback is the mechanism through which learning is confirmed, corrected, and extended. In educational games, feedback design is often the single most consequential variable: a well-designed game with poor feedback teaches less than a mediocre game with excellent feedback.

The research literature distinguishes several feedback types by their informational content:

**Knowledge of Results (KR)** tells the learner only whether their answer was correct or incorrect. It is the minimum feedback and, in isolation, the least effective: it confirms success or flags failure but provides no information about why or what to do differently.

**Knowledge of Correct Response (KCR)** shows the correct answer alongside the result. It is more effective than KR for acquisition — the learner at least knows what they should have said — but remains limited in its ability to support understanding.

**Elaborated Feedback (EF)** explains why the answer was correct or incorrect, provides worked examples, hints at underlying concepts, or guides the learner toward the correct approach. EF is consistently the most effective feedback type, particularly for novice learners who cannot independently diagnose their errors. For children learning mathematics, elaborated feedback — "Not quite — remember, 7 × 8 means seven groups of eight. Can you count up seven groups?" — is categorically more valuable than a red cross.

The research on timing further complicates feedback design. The intuitive position — that immediate feedback is always best — is not unambiguously supported by the evidence. Immediate feedback is clearly preferable for novice learners in elementary contexts, where allowing errors to sit uncorrected risks their being encoded. But for more complex problem-solving, some research suggests that delayed feedback, which allows for self-correction attempts and metacognitive processing, produces better long-term retention. The practical resolution for primary mathematics games is to default to immediate elaborated feedback, while building in periodic reflection moments for higher-order problem-solving tasks.

**Stealth assessment**, Valerie Shute's concept from her 2013 MIT Press monograph, represents the ideal form of feedback in educational games: continuous, non-intrusive measurement of competency through gameplay behaviour, with dynamic adjustment of content in response to inferred competency. In a stealth assessment system, the learner experiences only the game; the assessment is invisible. The game is simultaneously an instructional experience and a measurement instrument.

Stealth assessment is technically demanding and requires sophisticated evidence-centred assessment design. None of the games on the platform under analysis implement it in full. But the concept serves as a useful benchmark against which to evaluate the feedback and assessment elements that are present.

## 2.7 Number Sense: The Mathematics-Specific Foundation

Before leaving the theoretical foundations, it is worth noting one domain of research that is specific to mathematics education rather than learning science more broadly: the development of number sense.

Number sense is the intuitive, flexible understanding of number that underlies all formal mathematics. It encompasses one-to-one correspondence, cardinality, ordinality, numerical magnitude comparison, and — critically for game design — the spatial representation of number. Robert Siegler and colleagues have established, across numerous studies, that children as young as three years old have a mental number line: a spatial representation of numbers in increasing order that serves as a foundation for numerical magnitude understanding, arithmetic estimation, and eventually algebraic reasoning.

Linear number line games — games in which players physically navigate along or place numbers on a linear scale — have among the strongest empirical support of any intervention in elementary mathematics research. Siegler and Ramani's studies found that brief exposure to linear number board games (as opposed to circular board games) produced significant gains in number sense, magnitude comparison, counting, and arithmetic — gains that persisted at follow-up assessment. The mechanism is the correspondence between physical movement along a linear path and the internalization of a linear mental number representation.

The platform under analysis includes a dedicated number line game, and this research will inform its analysis directly. But the number sense research also bears on every other game on the platform: how any game represents and manipulates numbers — whether linearly, spatially, symbolically, or abstractly — has implications for the mental representations it builds in young learners.
