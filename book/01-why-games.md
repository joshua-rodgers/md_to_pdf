# Chapter 1: Why Games? The Research Case for Game-Based Learning

---

There is a version of the argument for educational games that is purely intuitive: children like games, children resist arithmetic, therefore if we put arithmetic in a game, children will like arithmetic. This argument is not wrong. But it is thin, and it has licensed too much design that mistakes the surface properties of games — the visual noise, the score counter, the cheerful sound effects — for the substance of what makes games work.

The more useful argument runs deeper and requires a detour through evidence.

---

## 1.1 A Brief History of Educational Math Games

Mathematics education has incorporated games far longer than the digital era would suggest. In the 1960s and 70s, the "new math" reforms brought manipulatives, puzzles, and game structures into primary mathematics classrooms, drawing on the theoretical work of Jerome Bruner and Zoltán Dienes. The commercial board game market produced mathematical titles throughout the twentieth century — *Monopoly* as an inadvertent lesson in arithmetic and probability, *24 Game* as a directed calculation challenge, *Set* as a combinatorics and pattern recognition exercise.

The arrival of personal computers in schools in the early 1980s created an entirely new design space, and the first wave of educational math software — *Number Munchers*, *Math Blaster*, the Oregon Trail's incidental arithmetic — established a template that persisted for decades: wrap calculation practice in mild entertainment and call it a game. The limitations of that template became clear quickly. Children enjoyed the games. Whether they learned durable mathematics from them was a separate question, and the research answer was frequently "not very much, and not for very long."

The field has since matured considerably. The past two decades have produced a substantial body of rigorous research, including randomised controlled trials, longitudinal studies, and meta-analyses that allow for measured conclusions about what educational games can and cannot do.

---

## 1.2 What the Meta-Analyses Tell Us

The most rigorous recent synthesis of the evidence is Barz et al. (2024), a meta-analysis of school-based digital game-based learning interventions published in the *American Educational Research Journal*. Across a large body of studies, the analysis found an overall learning effect of *g* = 0.54 — a moderate effect size that places well-designed educational games comfortably above no intervention and broadly comparable to other active learning approaches. For cognitive learning specifically, the effect size rises to *g* = 0.67, which is substantial.

In STEM subjects, the picture is similarly encouraging. A 2022 meta-analysis in the *International Journal of STEM Education*, drawing on 136 effect sizes from 86 studies, found a pooled effect of *g* = 0.624 favouring digital game-based learning over conventional instruction. For early childhood, a 2024 systematic review in *Frontiers in Psychology* confirmed that game-based learning promotes cognitive, social, and emotional development in young children — while adding an important caveat: not all games produce these effects, and educational alignment and developmental appropriateness are critical moderating factors.

The headline findings are worth holding in mind throughout this book: the research supports educational games as a category. The variation within that category — between well-designed and poorly designed games, between games whose mechanics are genuinely integrated with their learning objectives and games that merely dress up worksheets in interactive clothing — is enormous.

## 1.3 Games vs. Gamification: A Critical Distinction

Before proceeding, it is worth establishing a distinction that matters considerably for this analysis.

*Game-based learning* (GBL) refers to using actual games — artefacts with rules, challenges, feedback loops, and some form of player agency — as the vehicle for instruction. The learning happens *through the game*, either because the game embodies the content (you cannot play without engaging with the mathematics) or because the game provides a motivating context for practice.

*Gamification* refers to applying game-like elements — points, badges, leaderboards, progress bars, timers — to activities that are not fundamentally games. A worksheet that awards stars for correct answers is not a game; it is a gamified worksheet.

This distinction is not merely semantic. The research on GBL and the research on gamification are separate bodies of work with different findings. Gamification of learning shows more mixed results: a 2019 meta-analysis in *Educational Psychology Review* found gamification to be effective overall, but identified significant variance related to how gamification elements were implemented and whether they supported or undermined learner autonomy. The risks of gamification — demotivation from public leaderboards, the erosion of intrinsic interest by extrinsic reward structures, the novelty effect that produces early engagement followed by dropout — are well-documented and will be examined in detail in Chapter 4.

The platform under analysis sits across this distinction in interesting ways. Some of its games are genuine games; some are closer to gamified practice interfaces. Part of what this book aims to do is identify which is which, and what follows from that identification.

## 1.4 The Endogenous vs. Exogenous Fantasy Problem

The most important single idea in educational game design research was articulated by Malone and Lepper in 1987, and it has lost none of its relevance in the decades since. Malone and Lepper distinguished between two types of fantasy in educational games:

**Endogenous fantasy** is fantasy in which the content to be learned *is* the fantasy. The game mechanics and the learning content are inseparable: you cannot play without engaging with the mathematics, and engaging with the mathematics *is* how you play. The prototypical example they offered was a game about learning to navigate by the stars — you must understand the mathematics of celestial navigation in order to do anything at all.

**Exogenous fantasy** is fantasy in which the game world is a motivating wrapper around content that exists independently of it. The player answers arithmetic questions to earn in-game currency, or solves equations to unlock narrative progression. The mathematics and the fantasy are separable: you could, in principle, replace the mathematics with vocabulary definitions or historical dates without fundamentally changing the game.

Malone and Lepper argued — and subsequent researchers have largely confirmed — that endogenous games produce better learning transfer because the learning content is structurally embedded in the game mechanics rather than incidentally attached to them. When the mathematics is what you are doing, rather than what you must do before you can do what you are doing, it is processed differently. It becomes consequential.

Habgood, Ainsworth, and Benford formalised this argument in a 2005 paper in *Simulation and Gaming*, introducing the concept of "intrinsic integration" — the degree to which learning content is embedded in the core gameplay loop rather than sitting alongside it. Their empirical tests showed that intrinsically integrated games produced superior learning outcomes to extrinsically integrated equivalents, even when the surface features and time-on-task were identical.

This distinction will recur throughout Part II. Several games on the platform achieve genuine endogenous integration; others, despite being engaging and well-built, remain fundamentally exogenous. The chapter analyses will make these distinctions explicit.

## 1.5 The National Council of Teachers of Mathematics on Games

It is worth noting that the value of games in mathematics education is not a position held only by researchers working in educational technology. The National Council of Teachers of Mathematics (NCTM) — the primary professional body for mathematics educators in the United States — has published clear guidance on the role of games in fact fluency development:

> "Research about how students develop fact mastery indicates that drill techniques and timed tests do not have the power that mathematical games and other experiences have."

This is a striking statement. Drill and timed testing have been the dominant mode of arithmetic fact instruction in English-speaking schools for generations. The NCTM's position, backed by a substantial body of research, is that they are not the most effective tools available. Games — specifically because they embed arithmetic in a context that provides meaningful decision-making, social interaction, and variable challenge — outperform drill in producing durable fact recall.

The implication for educational game designers is significant: the goal is not to gamify drill. The goal is to replace drill with something structurally richer.

## 1.6 When Educational Games Fail: The Most Common Mistakes

If the research is broadly supportive of educational games, why does the market contain so many games that demonstrably do not work? The field has identified a fairly consistent set of failure modes.

**The drill wrapper.** The most common failure: arithmetic problems presented as a quiz, wrapped in a visual theme and a score counter. The game mechanics and the mathematical content are entirely separate. The "game" is decorative. Children engage briefly with the novelty and then disengage, having practised arithmetic no more effectively than a paper worksheet would have produced.

**Extraneous cognitive load.** Educational games often layer on visual complexity, sound, animation, and narrative in the belief that more stimulation produces more engagement. The opposite is frequently true. Cognitive Load Theory, which we will examine in depth in Chapter 2, is unambiguous: irrelevant stimulation competes for limited working memory resources and actively impedes learning. A game that is too visually noisy teaches nothing, regardless of how mathematically aligned its content is.

**The difficulty cliff.** Without adaptive systems, games present fixed difficulty levels. Children who advance too quickly become bored; children who progress too slowly become anxious and give up. Both states are inimical to learning. The research on flow theory (Chapter 2) demonstrates that learning is most effective when challenge is precisely calibrated to current ability — a state almost impossible to maintain with fixed difficulty levels for a heterogeneous group of learners.

**Undermining intrinsic motivation.** As we will examine in Chapter 4, the external reward structures common in gamification — points, leaderboards, badges — can actively undermine the intrinsic motivation that educators are trying to cultivate. The overjustification effect, documented by Deci from the early 1970s onward, shows that providing extrinsic rewards for intrinsically interesting activities reduces interest in those activities when the rewards are removed. Games designed around points-for-answers may produce short-term engagement while systematically eroding long-term mathematical interest.

**The assessment gap.** Many educational games make it impossible to distinguish engaged performance from correct guessing, test-and-retry from genuine understanding, or fluency with a specific item type from transferable mathematical knowledge. Without embedded assessment — or what Valerie Shute terms "stealth assessment" — the game generates no useful information about whether learning has occurred.

**Developmental mismatch.** A game designed for eight-year-olds, deployed with six-year-olds, will often fail — not because the mathematics is too difficult, but because the interface assumptions, the literacy demands, the attention span requirements, or the motor control required do not match the developmental stage of the user. Children are not small adults, and the differences between a four-year-old and a six-year-old are more significant, in terms of cognitive and developmental stage, than the differences between a twenty-five-year-old and a forty-year-old.

These are the failure modes we will be looking for — and, where we find them, naming clearly — throughout Part II.
