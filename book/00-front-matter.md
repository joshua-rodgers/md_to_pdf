# Learning Through Play
## An Expert Design Analysis of an Educational Math Games Platform

---

### About This Book

This book is a design critique — rigorous, specific, and, where the evidence warrants it, enthusiastic.

Its subject is a collection of browser-based mathematics games built for primary school learners. The platform covers addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, fractions, place value, and estimation across a span roughly equivalent to Kindergarten through Grade 6. Some of its games are elegant. Some are clever. Some have real problems. All of them are worth examining closely, because close examination is how the field of educational game design gets better.

The analysis is structured as a sustained dialogue between two expert perspectives. **Dr. Ruth Cohn** is an educational researcher with a specialism in digital game-based learning, constructivist pedagogy, and K–12 mathematics curriculum design. Her central question is always: *does this design produce durable, transferable learning?* **Farai Dube** is a senior game developer with a decade of experience in educational software, UX architecture for children's products, and interactive systems design. His central question is always: *does this design produce genuine engagement — and does it sustain it?*

These two perspectives are not always in agreement. When Cohn sees a timer as an anxiety trigger, Dube may see it as a necessary pacing mechanism. When Dube admires a visual reward system, Cohn may point to research showing it undermines intrinsic motivation. The disagreements are productive. The field needs both lenses.

The book is structured in three parts. Part I establishes the theoretical and design foundations — the learning science, the game design frameworks, the research on gamification, and the UX principles specific to young learners — that inform every chapter that follows. Part II is a game-by-game analysis: ten case studies, each applying the frameworks to the actual mechanics of a specific game on the platform. Part III steps back to ask the larger questions: do the games work as a curriculum? What is missing? And what should be built next?

---

### A Note on Scope

This book does not claim to comprehensively review the market for educational math games. The platform under analysis is one product made by one educator-developer, and the analysis is directed at that product. Where we reference other platforms (Prodigy, Khan Academy Kids, Mathletics, ST Math), it is by way of comparison, not comprehensive review.

We have also chosen to exclude the platform's reading companion application from this analysis. That product has a different design philosophy, a different target audience, and different research literature behind it. It deserves its own treatment.

### Meet the Analysts

**Dr. Ruth Cohn** trained in developmental psychology before completing a doctorate in educational technology, with a dissertation on feedback design in mathematics software. She has spent the last fifteen years working at the intersection of instructional design theory and classroom practice, consulting for school districts and curriculum publishers, and publishing research on game-based learning in peer-reviewed journals. She comes to this analysis as someone who believes deeply in the potential of games to transform mathematics education — and who has watched that potential squandered too many times by well-intentioned designs that prioritise engagement theatre over genuine learning.

**Farai Dube** began his career building mobile games for commercial publishers before a pivot — part professional, part moral — toward educational software. He has shipped seven educational products for children aged four through twelve, led UX research with hundreds of child participants, and spent years arguing inside product teams that "kid-friendly" and "pedagogically sound" are not opposites. He approaches this analysis with the conviction that a bad UX can ruin a good learning design, and a good UX cannot save a bad one.

They have not always agreed with each other during the writing of this book. Those disagreements are preserved in the text.

### How to Read This Book

Part I is foundational. Readers already fluent in cognitive load theory, flow theory, and self-determination theory may skim the corresponding sections, using them as a reference for the frameworks applied in Part II. Readers new to educational game design research will benefit from reading Part I in full before proceeding.

Part II can be read selectively. Each case study chapter is substantially self-contained; a reader interested specifically in fraction instruction can read Chapter 16 without having read Chapters 7 through 15. Cross-references are provided where the games share design patterns or contrast instructively.

Part III should be read in sequence.

The appendices provide condensed reference material: a learning theory glossary, a RETAIN evaluation scorecard applied to each game, a consolidated list of design recommendations, and a curated further reading list.
