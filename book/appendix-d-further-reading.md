# Appendix D: Further Reading and Key Sources

---

This appendix lists the primary sources cited in the book, organised by topic area. URLs are provided where the source is openly accessible.

---

## Game-Based Learning: Foundations and Meta-Analyses

**Barz, N. et al. (2024).** A meta-analysis of school-based digital game-based learning interventions. *American Educational Research Journal.* The most comprehensive recent synthesis of the effectiveness evidence for digital GBL in school settings.

**Plass, J.L., Homer, B.D., & Kinzer, C.K. (2015).** Foundations of game-based learning. *Educational Psychologist, 50*(4), 258–283. The most cited theoretical synthesis of GBL research; introduces the multidimensional engagement model. Open access: https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1090277.pdf

**Malone, T., & Lepper, M. (1987).** Making learning fun: A taxonomy of intrinsic motivations for learning. In R. Snow & M. Farr (Eds.), *Aptitude, Learning, and Instruction*. Introduces the endogenous/exogenous fantasy distinction and the taxonomy of intrinsic motivations.

**Habgood, M.P.J., Ainsworth, S., & Benford, S. (2005).** Endogenous fantasy and learning in digital games. *Simulation and Gaming, 36*(4), 483–498. The primary empirical test of endogenous vs. exogenous integration in educational games.

**Frontiers in Education (2024).** Game-based learning in primary mathematics: Systematic review. https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2024.1331312/full

---

## Learning Theory

**Sweller, J. (1988).** Cognitive load during problem solving: Effects on learning. *Cognitive Science, 12*, 257–285. The foundational CLT paper.

**Ryan, R.M., & Deci, E.L. (2000).** Self-determination theory and the facilitation of intrinsic motivation, social development, and well-being. *American Psychologist, 55*(1), 68–78. The primary SDT overview paper.

**Csikszentmihalyi, M. (1990).** *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience.* Harper. The foundational work on flow theory.

**Chen, J. (2007).** Flow in games (and everything else). *Communications of the ACM, 50*(4), 31–34. The operationalisation of flow for game design. MFA thesis available at https://www.jenovachen.com/flowingames/Flow_in_games_final.pdf

**Vygotsky, L.S. (1978).** *Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes.* Harvard University Press.

---

## Math-Specific Research

**Siegler, R.S., & Ramani, G.B.** Research on linear number board games and number sense development. Key papers span 2008–2012 in *Developmental Psychology* and *Psychological Science.*

**Siegler, R.S. et al. (2012).** Early predictors of high school mathematics achievement. *Psychological Science, 23*(7), 691–697. Demonstrates that fractions and division understanding in Grade 5 is the strongest predictor of high school mathematics achievement.

**NCTM (National Council of Teachers of Mathematics).** Why play math games? https://www.nctm.org/Publications/TCM-blog/Blog/Why-Play-Math-Games_/

**MDPI Education Sciences (2025).** A conceptual model for anxiety-reducing mathematics games. https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/16/1/34

---

## Gamification and Motivation

**Deci, E.L. (1971).** Effects of externally mediated rewards on intrinsic motivation. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 18*(1), 105–115. The foundational overjustification effect study.

**Hanus, M.D., & Fox, J. (2015).** Assessing the effects of gamification in the classroom: A longitudinal study on intrinsic motivation, social comparison, satisfaction, effort, and academic performance. *Computers & Education, 80*, 152–161.

**Landers, R.N. et al. (2017).** Gamification of task performance with leaderboards: A goal setting experiment. *Computers in Human Behavior, 71*, 508–515.

**Educational Psychology Review (2019).** Gamification as a tool for instructional design. https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-019-09498-w (meta-analysis)
